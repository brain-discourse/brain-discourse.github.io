## 7a. Technical brainstorm — deep-dive answers for surprise questions

These are honest limitations of your take-home and mechanistic questions Chris could reasonably ask given his own genomics-research background. Know the shape of each answer; you don't need to recite all of it.

**1. Context sequence (G\>T) and GC-rich regions — dosage not assessed.** G\>T/C\>A transversions are the signature of 8-oxoguanine oxidative DNA damage introduced during library prep (shearing/sonication), not true biological variants — flagged in somatic/FFPE pipelines via orientation-bias filtering (e.g., D-toxoG), less commonly modeled explicitly in standard germline calling. GC-rich/GC-poor regions get uneven coverage from PCR-based library prep (PCR-free prep largely fixes this) — a distinct mechanism from homopolymer slippage that your report didn't stratify by. Bigger honest gap: your whole analysis assumes diploid (CN=2) throughout. You never assessed local copy number/dosage, so in a true deletion (CN=1) or duplication (CN=3+) region, the diploid genotype-likelihood model GATK/Strelka2 use is simply wrong there — allele balance and het/hom calls both become misleading without a CNV layer to condition on.

**2. Why telomeric regions are hard.** Long near-identical (TTAGGG)n repeats and subtelomeric segmental duplications collapse short-read mappability to near zero; reference assembly gaps/incompleteness near telomeres compound this (even GRCh38, worse before T2T-CHM13); it's the same low-mappability/segdup mechanism from your Step 4 findings, just at its most extreme.

**3. Ploidy in GATK.** HaplotypeCaller defaults to `--sample-ploidy 2`; you override it for haploid contexts (chrY, mito, male chrX non-PAR) or higher-ploidy pooled/tumor samples. Ploidy sets how many alt-allele dosage states the genotype-likelihood model considers — get it wrong in a CNV region and het/hom calls come out wrong, same root issue as question 1.

**4. gVCF vs. genotyped VCF.** A gVCF records every position — variant or reference-confidence block — so it distinguishes "confident reference" from "no data," which a normal VCF doesn't. This is what makes joint genotyping across cohorts possible (CombineGVCFs/GenomicsDBImport → GenotypeGVCFs) without re-touching the original BAMs. A genotyped/final VCF only lists called sites relative to the reference.

**5. Phasing to improve benchmarking; is LD useful?** Phasing assigns variants to parental haplotypes — needed to distinguish compound-het (trans) from cis configuration, which changes clinical interpretation entirely for recessive disease. It adds a benchmarking axis hap.py doesn't score (switch-error rate vs a phased truth set). LD is the whole mechanism behind statistical phasing (Eagle2/SHAPEIT4/Beagle borrow haplotype structure from a reference panel) — valuable for germline. It does **not** transfer to somatic variants: a somatic mutation isn't an inherited population polymorphism, so there's no population LD to exploit; somatic phasing needs read-backed or long-read phasing instead.

**6. Can a gVCF detect SVs/CNVs?** Not directly — gVCFs are a small-variant data structure. Depth-based CNV callers (CNVkit, GATK gCNV) use binned read-depth ratios from the BAM, not gVCF records. Discordant-pair/split-read SV callers (Manta, DELLY, LUMPY) need direct BAM access to read orientation/insert size — none of that survives into a gVCF. What a gVCF *can* hint at: unexpected allele-balance/heterozygosity anomalies that should trigger dedicated CNV/SV calling, but it can't call breakpoints itself.

**7. Predicting pathogenicity for a novel VUS not in ClinVar.** Fall back to ACMG/AMP evidence tiers that don't require prior clinical curation: population absence/rarity in gnomAD (PM2), in-silico predictors (REVEL, CADD, AlphaMissense, SpliceAI — PP3/BP4), conservation/domain context (PM1), segregation in affected relatives (PP1), confirmed de novo status (PM6/PS2), functional assay evidence if available (PS3/BS3). Without curated clinical evidence you're typically capped at VUS unless enough independent lines converge — and functional/orthogonal validation (in this Heinzen-lab context, ddPCR-confirmed VAF) is often the deciding step.

**8. How do PolyPhen-2 / SnpSift scores work?** PolyPhen-2 is a naive Bayes classifier over conservation (PSIC from multiple alignment), structural context (solvent accessibility/secondary structure where a PDB structure exists), and Pfam domain hits — outputs benign/possibly/probably-damaging with a probability. SnpSift itself computes nothing — it's an annotation/filtering engine (SnpEff suite) that pulls in external scores (dbNSFP: SIFT/PolyPhen/CADD/REVEL, ClinVar, gnomAD) for you to filter on; don't conflate the two. CADD trains a model to separate simulated de novo variants from variants that survived selection, integrating many annotations into one continuous deleteriousness score.

**9. Do the same FP/FN patterns transfer to a WGS dataset from a different kit?** Partially. The mappability/reference-structure-driven part of your finding (homopolymers, segdups, low-mappability regions) generalizes across platforms because it's about the genome, not the assay. Kit-specific artifacts (capture bias, PCR-based vs PCR-free GC bias, platform-specific systematic error motifs, insert size/read length effects on indel calling) do not transfer — you'd need to re-run hap.py against GIAB truth on the new kit's own data to get calibrated numbers. Good one-liner: "context effects generalize, magnitudes and platform artifacts don't."

**10. How does GATK assign PASS/FAIL?** Two paths: hard filtering on annotations (QD, FS, MQ, MQRankSum, ReadPosRankSum, SOR) against GATK Best Practices thresholds — what you almost certainly used here, since VQSR needs a large multi-sample cohort to train its Gaussian-mixture model reliably and this was a single HG002 sample. VQSR is the alternative for large cohorts: trains on known-true variant sets (HapMap/Omni/1000G) and assigns a VQSLOD score with tranche-based sensitivity thresholds.

**11. AD vs. DP — which is more accurate?** They answer different questions, not a strict better/worse. DP = total read depth (coverage/power). AD = per-allele depth (ref, alt counts) — this is what lets you compute allele balance, the actual quantity that flags artifacts (your Step 5 analysis). Use DP to judge whether you had enough data; use AD to judge whether the genotype call itself is trustworthy. Worth knowing FORMAT DP can differ slightly from sum(AD) due to filtered/low-quality bases counted differently.

**12. Can LD be used with germline calls? What about somatic?** Germline — yes, that's the entire basis of statistical phasing, imputation, and fine-mapping (population-level allele-frequency correlation from shared ancestry/limited recombination). Somatic — no, not in the population-genetics sense: a single post-zygotic mutation has no population allele-frequency structure. The analogous concept in somatic biology is **clonal co-occurrence** — which mutations appear on the same reads/molecules or in the same single cells, revealing subclonal lineage structure — mechanistically similar to read-backed phasing, not LD.

**13. Common SNV/CNV/SV detection strategies — assumptions and limits (corrected/sharpened).** SNV/indel: local-reassembly callers (GATK HaplotypeCaller, de Bruijn graph) assume diploid germline and are poorly tuned for low-VAF somatic; statistical/pileup callers (Strelka2, VarScan2) are faster with calibrated error models but still diploid-assuming in germline mode; CNN/pileup-image callers (DeepVariant) rely less on hand-tuned heuristics but need representative training data.

CNV — the real mechanism is usually an **HMM over binned read-depth**, not just thresholding: GATK's GermlineCNVCaller and XHMM model copy-number state (0,1,2,3,4+...) as a hidden Markov chain, where each bin's normalized depth ratio is an emission and the transition probabilities enforce spatial contiguity (real CNVs are stretches, so neighboring bins should share state, not flip randomly). Not every depth-based tool is HMM-based, though — CNVkit segments with circular binary segmentation (CBS) instead. Either way these need GC/mappability-normalized depth and benefit from a matched normal/panel of normals; poor resolution for small CNVs regardless of segmentation method. B-allele-frequency methods (heterozygous SNP allele-ratio) catch copy-neutral LOH that pure depth-based methods miss entirely, but need heterozygous SNPs present in the region.

SV — don't lump all short-read callers into one "discordant-pair/split-read" bucket, the mechanisms differ: BreakDancer is discordant-read-pair clustering only (coarse breakpoints, no split-read refinement); Pindel is split-read/pattern-growth (precise breakpoints, needs one side to anchor uniquely); **DELLY explicitly does both in sequence** — discordant pairs first cluster and type the SV (deletion/duplication/inversion/translocation), then split-reads refine the breakpoint to base-pair precision; LUMPY doesn't stage this at all, it's a unified probabilistic framework treating discordant-pair/split-read/read-depth as separate evidence-type distributions combined into one breakpoint likelihood; Manta uses local assembly plus split-read/discordant-pair evidence for both germline and somatic. All of these fail in repetitive/duplicated breakpoint regions (same mappability problem as everywhere else in this doc). Long-read callers (Sniffles, pbsv on PacBio/ONT) span repeats and large SVs directly with much better sensitivity, at the cost of a different (now HiFi-level low) long-read error profile; assembly-based SV calling is most accurate for novel/complex events but computationally expensive.

## 7b. Bioalgorithm cheat sheet — framed for a CS-major interviewer

Chris is CS+Biochem (Berkeley), and this is an engineering-flavored role. He's less likely to ask "what does BWA do" and more likely to ask "what data structure/algorithm makes that fast, and where does the assumption break." Table form for fast scanning:

| Tool | Core algorithm / data structure | Assumption / where it breaks |
|------------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| BWA-MEM / BWA-MEM2 | FM-index (BWT-based compressed suffix array) for exact seed lookup, then seed-and-extend with banded affine-gap Smith-Waterman | Assumes read origin is a small edit distance from reference; degrades on divergent/rearranged regions; can't resolve true multi-mapping in repeats, only score it |
| minimap2 | Minimizer sketching (smallest k-mer hash per window) for sparse approximate seeding tolerant of long-read error, then colinear chaining (DP) + base-level extension | Seed sparsity can miss short exons/variants in low-complexity regions |
| GATK HaplotypeCaller | Local de Bruijn graph assembly of the active region to enumerate candidate haplotypes, then PairHMM forward algorithm (probabilistic DP) for read-haplotype likelihoods, then Bayesian genotype posterior | Diploid assumption; repeats/homopolymers create cycles/ambiguous paths in the graph itself — that's the actual mechanistic reason those regions fail, not just "low coverage" |
| Strelka2 | Simpler local realignment (not full graph assembly) + calibrated statistical model with an empirically fit indel-error model | Faster than GATK; potentially less sensitive to complex nested indels that need true haplotype reconstruction |
| DeepVariant | Pileup encoded as a multi-channel image tensor, CNN (Inception-v3-style) classifies genotype | No explicit biological model — failures are less interpretable; needs representative training data, domain-shift risk on unusual chemistries |
| hap.py / vcfeval | Variant normalization + DP-based sequence comparison to resolve differently-represented indels describing the same haplotype | Solves representation ambiguity; complex multi-allelic regions can still misassign TP/FP/FN |
| SHAPEIT4 / Eagle2 / Beagle | Li-Stephens HMM (hidden state = which reference haplotype you're "copying") + PBWT (positional Burrows-Wheeler transform, Durbin 2014) for near-linear-time haplotype matching at biobank scale | Degrades for rare variants/haplotypes underrepresented in the reference panel — real ancestry-representation limitation |
| LDSC | Linear regression of GWAS χ² on LD Scores, summary-stats only | Assumes LD panel matches study ancestry; intercept diagnoses stratification vs true polygenicity |
| PLINK / BOLT-LMM / SAIGE | Per-SNP regression, or a mixed model adding a genetic-relationship-matrix random effect, made tractable via conjugate-gradient/randomized linear algebra instead of inverting an N×N matrix | Real numerical-engineering-at-scale problem — good one to bring up unprompted, it's the most "CS" item on this list |
| samtools/htslib (BAM/CRAM) | BGZF blocked-gzip for random access + BAI/CSI binning index (R-tree-like) for \~O(log n) region queries; CRAM adds reference-based delta compression | CRAM needs the exact reference for decompression — an operational dependency people forget |
| kallisto / Salmon | k-mer hash / de Bruijn-graph read-to-transcript compatibility classes (skips full alignment) + EM to resolve multi-mapping into abundance | Depends on a complete transcriptome annotation; can't discover/quantify unannotated novel isoforms |
| ANNOVAR / VEP / SnpEff | Interval-tree / binned-interval lookups (same genomic-range-indexing idea as tabix) + consequence-priority ranking per transcript | Transcript-model dependent (RefSeq/Ensembl/GENCODE disagree); canonical-transcript choice can miss real consequences on other isoforms |
| GATK gCNV / XHMM | HMM over binned, normalized read depth — transition probabilities enforce spatial contiguity of copy-number state | Needs GC/mappability normalization and ideally a panel of normals; poor resolution for small CNVs |
| VQSR | Gaussian mixture model fit via EM over multivariate call-quality annotations, trained against known-true variant sets | Needs many samples/variants to fit reliably — degenerate on small cohorts, which is why your single-HG002-sample run should use hard filtering instead |

## 7c. Engineering-specific prep — what a "genomics bench" team actually builds

I pulled the real structure of Latch's public scBench repo (Apache-2.0, github.com/latchbio/scbench) to ground this instead of guessing. Use it directly — it's almost certainly the template your team's "VariantBench" is built from.

**What scBench actually is, concretely:** - 394 verifiable problems, each = a data snapshot (an `.h5ad` file) + a natural-language task prompt + a deterministic grader that maps the agent's structured output to pass/fail. - Only a small "canonical examples" subset (one per task category) is public; the full 394-eval set is deliberately withheld **to prevent training contamination** — if eval questions leak into a future model's pretraining corpus, the model can "know the answer" without doing the analysis, which invalidates the benchmark. This is a live, real concern in the field (dataset decontamination), not a hypothetical — good to name-drop if asked why you'd keep some of your own MCQs private in a real deployment. - Five grader families, each suited to a different answer shape: NumericTolerance (QC metrics/counts), MultipleChoice (interpretation questions — this is what your take-home MCQs map onto), MarkerGenePrecisionRecall (P\@K/R\@K for gene/variant lists), LabelSetJaccard (cell-type or category sets), DistributionComparison (proportions). - The harness (execution environment/tool access/scaffolding around the model) is a first-class engineering artifact, not an afterthought: Latch's own SpatialBench work showed the *same* base model (Opus 4.5) scoring 38.4% bare, 48.1% under Claude Code, and 61.7% under Latch's own harness — a 23-point swing from harness design alone, independent of the model. This is probably the single most important "engineering" fact to have ready: the model is not the bottleneck, the surrounding infrastructure is. - Package is a real piece of software: `pyproject.toml`, a `scbench` CLI (`scbench validate`, `scbench run --agent minisweagent --model ...`), a pluggable custom-agent interface (a Python function taking `task_prompt`/`work_dir`, writing a structured `eval_answer.json`), CONTRIBUTING.md, results with 95% CIs and latency per model.

**Questions Chris (CS-major, engineering-flavored interview) may ask that connect directly to this:** - "How would you turn one of your take-home MCQs into an automated grader like scBench's?" → Your MCQs map straight onto the `MultipleChoice` grader family. ## 7d. GIAB across kits, PON provenance, and defining "noise" vs "real"

**Can GIAB be reused for a different kit?** Yes, and this is standard practice — but be precise about *why*, because it's a common point of confusion. GIAB's truth VCF and high-confidence BED are constructed from an arbitration/consensus across many technologies (short-read, long-read, linked-read, optical/array) and are genome-wide, kit-agnostic ground truth about HG002's actual genotype — they don't encode anything about a particular capture kit or chemistry. What changes per kit is the **callable region**: for an exome or targeted panel, you intersect the kit's own target/capture BED with GIAB's high-confidence BED, and only benchmark inside that intersection — outside the capture region you have no data (not applicable), and outside GIAB's HC region even where you do have data, the truth label itself isn't trustworthy. hap.py/vcfeval support exactly this restriction via the `-f`/target-BED arguments.

**Do you need the actual reference material re-sequenced on the new kit, or can you reuse existing GIAB BAMs?** You need real data generated from the new kit. NIST distributes the physical DNA reference material (e.g., NA24385 for HG002, via Coriell) precisely so any lab can run it through their own assay and get a kit-representative benchmark — this is exactly what CAP/CLIA analytical validation protocols require for a new clinical assay. You cannot repurpose the original GIAB WGS BAMs (sequenced on a different platform/kit) and claim they represent your new kit's error profile; the ground truth transfers, the error profile does not.

**Panel of Normals (PON) — sequenced controls, or can it be synthetic?** A PON (used most in somatic calling, e.g., Mutect2) exists to capture the *empirical, assay-specific* artifact/noise profile — recurrent low-VAF technical noise and mapping artifacts specific to a kit/chemistry/capture design/pipeline version. That is fundamentally a measured property of a real wet-lab process, so real sequenced normal samples (ideally same kit, same lab, same pipeline version) are the gold standard — there's no way around actually measuring your own assay's noise floor if you want a PON that reflects it. Two practical fallbacks, both with real limitations you should state plainly rather than oversell: (1) public aggregated PONs (e.g., Broad's Mutect2 PON built from large public normal cohorts) catch broad genome-wide artifact-prone sites but miss your kit's specific systematic biases unless it happens to share chemistry; (2) fully synthetic/simulated reads (ART, pbsim-style error models) are useful for controlled algorithm benchmarking but don't reproduce real wet-lab artifacts (chimera formation, real flow-cell-specific error patterns, actual PCR-duplication/GC-bias interactions), so they're a poor substitute for a true PON. Bottom line: synthetic PONs can bootstrap you before you have your own cohort, but they are an approximation, not equivalent.

**How do you actually calculate an artifact/noise rate?** Several converging methods, not just one: (1) technical replicates of the same DNA — any signal not reproducible across replicates at the same site is very likely noise; (2) known-truth benchmarking (GIAB + hap.py, exactly what you did) gives a direct empirical FP rate per caller/kit; (3) duplicate-marking output (samtools markdup/Picard MarkDuplicates) directly reports PCR duplication rate, a distinct noise source from GC bias; (4) background error-rate modeling from invariant reference sites, stratified by read cycle/position, dinucleotide context, and machine/lane — this is literally what BQSR does, and it's a formal way of separating expected sequencing error from real variant signal; (5) for low-VAF/somatic work specifically, the background noise floor at a given position can be measured empirically across a panel of normals, and a call is only trusted if its observed VAF significantly exceeds that floor — the statistical logic behind PON filtering and ultra-low-VAF duplex-sequencing methods.

**What distinguishes real signal from noise, mechanistically:** real variants are reproducible across independent preps/replicates, show allele-balance consistent with expected zygosity/clonal fraction, are supported on **both strands** roughly proportionally, and ideally are confirmed on an orthogonal platform (Sanger/ddPCR/long-read). Noise/artifacts tend to be strand-specific (the classic diagnostic — e.g., 8-oxoG oxidative damage and polymerase-slippage errors are often single-strand-supported, unlike true hets), clustered at read-end positions (3′ quality dropoff), supported only by low-mapping-quality or soft-clipped reads, and — the clearest PON-style signature — recurrent at the exact same position across many unrelated samples regardless of biology.

**Beyond GC bias — other coverage-noise factors:** mappability/repeat content (down-weighted or ambiguous multi-mapping reads create artificial dips); PCR duplication rate and library complexity (low-complexity/over-amplified libraries redundantly resequence the same molecules); fragmentation method and insert-size uniformity (sonication vs. enzymatic fragmentation have different site-preference biases); sequencer/flow-cell/reagent-lot batch effects, including index/barcode hopping causing low-level cross-sample contamination in multiplexed runs; capture/hybridization efficiency for exome/panel kits (bait density, repeat-masking of probes, secondary structure — a superset of plain GC content); reference bias (alt-supporting reads, especially indel-spanning ones, align slightly worse than ref-matching reads, systematically under-representing alt support — partially mitigated by graph-genome or personalized-reference realignment); sample-level issues like DNA degradation (common in FFPE) and physical cross-contamination, which is why contamination estimation (e.g., VerifyBamID's freemix statistic from allele-balance patterns at known polymorphic sites) is a standard QC step; and NUMTs (nuclear-embedded mitochondrial-like sequences) causing a specific cross-mapping artifact near mitochondrial-homologous regions.

## 7e. GRIDSS/GRIPSS, statistical vs read-backed phasing, BQSR mechanics, precision/recall, local alignment, ancestry/zygosity/duplication/sex QC, MoChA

**GRIDSS / GRIPSS (missing from the earlier SV list — add these).** GRIDSS takes an assembly-based approach rather than pure discordant-pair/split-read clustering (Manta/DELLY/LUMPY): it performs positional de Bruijn graph assembly of soft-clipped/split reads and discordant pairs locally around each candidate breakend, then aligns the resulting contigs back to the reference for base-pair-precise breakpoints — the same "local reassembly" idea as HaplotypeCaller, applied to breakends instead of small variants. It represents everything in breakend (BND) notation rather than symbolic DEL/DUP/INV types, which handles complex/chained rearrangements better than a fixed SV-type vocabulary. GRIPSS is the companion filtering tool (Hartwig Medical Foundation pipeline, paired with PURPLE for purity/ploidy and LINX for interpretation) that applies tumor/normal comparison, PON filtering, and quality filters to raw GRIDSS calls to produce a high-confidence somatic SV set. Tradeoff: assembly is more compute-expensive and still breaks down at very low coverage or extremely repetitive breakpoint junctions.

**SHAPEIT-style statistical phasing vs. WhatsHap read-backed phasing — the actual difference.** Statistical/population phasing (SHAPEIT4/Eagle2/Beagle) works purely from unphased genotypes plus population haplotype structure (Li-Stephens HMM + PBWT, as covered earlier) — it never touches a sequencing read. Strength: works at any genomic distance (chromosome-scale), because it borrows information from the reference panel. Weakness: probabilistic — introduces switch errors, degrades for rare variants or ancestries underrepresented in the panel, and cannot phase a truly private/de novo variant that has no population-level LD signal to borrow from. Read-backed phasing (WhatsHap) instead uses direct physical evidence: if two heterozygous sites are both covered by the same read or read pair, that read *is* proof they're on the same physical molecule — no statistical model needed. WhatsHap formalizes this as the Minimum Error Correction (MEC) problem — find the two haplotypes requiring the fewest allele "corrections" to explain all reads — solved by dynamic programming (exact for bounded coverage). Strength: works for private/de novo variants regardless of population frequency, no reference panel needed. Weakness: only phases variants within a single fragment's span (\~300–500bp for short reads, so phase blocks break wherever no read spans two heterozygous sites) — long reads (HiFi/ONT) extend this dramatically, sometimes to chromosome scale. In practice these are complementary and often combined (WhatsHap has a mode that merges read-backed local phase blocks using population/pedigree information for long-range scaffolding).

**How BQSR (Base Quality Score Recalibration) actually happens, step by step.** (1) Start from raw, known-miscalibrated sequencer-reported Phred quality scores. (2) Mask out known-variant sites using a known-sites VCF (dbSNP/1000G) so real variants aren't mistaken for errors while building the model — this is why a known-sites file is a required BQSR input. (3) At all remaining presumed-reference-matching positions, tabulate the actual observed mismatch rate, stratified by covariates: reported quality score, cycle/position within the read, sequence context (preceding+current base), and read group/lane. (4) Fit an empirical model mapping each covariate combination to a real observed error rate. (5) Recalibrate: replace each base's original quality score with the value the empirical model says it should have, given its specific covariates — a base in a dinucleotide context/cycle with a systematically higher real error rate gets downgraded even if the machine reported high confidence, and vice versa. (6) The recalibrated BAM feeds into variant calling so per-base confidence weighting reflects true empirical error rather than the machine's raw estimate. GATK implements this as two steps: `BaseRecalibrator` builds the table, `ApplyBQSR` applies it.

**Precision/recall, defined precisely.** Precision = TP/(TP+FP) — of everything you called positive, what fraction is actually real; it's a trust/purity measure of your positive calls. Recall (sensitivity) = TP/(TP+FN) — of everything actually true, what fraction you successfully detected; it's a completeness measure. F1 is their harmonic mean (penalizes either one being low, unlike a plain average). In your report's exact context: precision = fraction of called variants matching GIAB truth; recall = fraction of GIAB truth variants you successfully called. Adjusting filter stringency trades one against the other — that's what your Step 3 precision-recall tradeoff plot visualizes.

**Where is local alignment specifically useful (vs. global)?** Local alignment (Smith-Waterman) is right whenever only *part* of a sequence is expected to match — reads that partially align because they span a breakpoint, carry adapter contamination, or need soft-clipping at low-quality ends; candidate-haplotype-to-reference alignment inside a variant caller (indel/complex-variant regions need local, not full-length, alignment); split-read SV detection, where different segments of one read/contig locally align to different genomic locations; and domain/motif search in proteins (BLAST-style), where only a conserved region matters, not the whole sequence. Global alignment (Needleman-Wunsch) is appropriate only when you expect end-to-end similarity across comparable-length sequences (e.g., two closely related full-length gene sequences) — forcing full-length alignment on sequences with unrelated flanking content produces misleading results.

**Assessing ancestry, zygosity, sample duplication, and sex directly from sequencing data.** - *Ancestry*: PCA on genome-wide common-variant genotypes, projected against a reference panel (1000 Genomes super-populations) — samples cluster by ancestry along the top PCs. Model-based alternatives: ADMIXTURE-style clustering assuming K ancestral populations with allele-frequency likelihood models, or RFMix-style local ancestry inference for admixed individuals. - *Zygosity*: directly from genotype calls, but confidence depends on allele balance (same AD-based logic as before) — expected het AB≈0.5, hom≈0 or 1; deviations flag either a miscall or an underlying CNV (a true single-copy deletion looks artificially homozygous; a duplication skews AB away from the diploid expectation) — this is the same dosage-blindness issue from earlier in this doc, showing up again from a different angle. - *Sample duplication / relatedness / mislabeling*: pairwise identity-by-descent (IBD)/kinship estimation across many common SNPs (KING, PLINK `--genome`, PC-Relate) — a kinship coefficient near 0.5 with near-complete genome-wide concordance means "two samples" are actually the same individual (duplicate or mislabeling); ~0.25/~0.125 etc. flag first-/second-degree relatives. Standard QC in any multi-sample cohort to catch sample swaps before they confound downstream analysis. - *Sex*: X-chromosome heterozygosity rate + X:autosome depth ratio (males are hemizygous on X outside the PAR — no het calls, \~half the autosomal depth; females show normal diploid het rate and \~1:1 depth ratio), plus Y-chromosome coverage (present = male, near-absent/noise-only = female). Tools: Peddy, PLINK sex-check, VerifyBamID. Discordance between reported and genetically inferred sex is a classic sample-swap red flag.

**How MoChA detects somatic mosaic LOH/CNV.** MoChA jointly models two signals across a chromosome/arm from SNP-array or sequencing genotype data: Log R Ratio (LRR, total signal intensity ≈ copy number, analogous to normalized depth) and B-allele frequency (BAF, fraction of signal from the alternate allele at heterozygous sites). A normal diploid het site sits at BAF≈0.5/LRR≈0. A **mosaic** event (present in only a fraction of cells, unlike a constitutional/fully clonal CNV) produces subtler, scaled deviations: mosaic loss → LRR decrease + BAF "wings" splitting away from 0.5 in proportion to the clonal cell fraction (less extreme than a full constitutional deletion, which would push BAF all the way to 0/1); mosaic gain → LRR increase + extra BAF banding beyond the normal 0/0.5/1 pattern; copy-neutral mosaic LOH → BAF wings with *no* LRR change (total copy number stays 2, but one parental haplotype is lost and the other duplicated within the affected clone). MoChA segments these joint LRR+BAF deviations with an HMM (same segmentation logic as gCNV/XHMM elsewhere in this doc), and critically uses **statistical haplotype phasing** (Eagle2/SHAPEIT-style, from earlier in this doc) to know which allele *should* move together across a whole chromosome arm if a mosaic event is present — this converts noisy independent per-SNP allelic-ratio signal into one coherent, phased, chromosome-arm-scale directional signal, which is what gives it sensitivity to detect mosaic events even at low cell fraction. Good full-circle callback if asked: this is the same phasing concept from earlier, applied to somatic/mosaic detection instead of germline benchmarking — directly relevant to your own dissertation work on somatic mosaicism.

## 7f. Calling germline/somatic variants and CNV from RNA-seq — how the workflow differs

Yes, it's done routinely, but it's a genuinely different problem from DNA-seq, not the same pipeline with a different input file. Six real differences, in the order they'd actually bite you:

**1. Alignment must be splice-aware.** Reads span exon-exon junctions, so you need STAR or HISAT2 (not BWA) — STAR's 2-pass mode detects novel splice junctions in a first pass, then rebuilds the index to align a second time against those junctions, which reduces spurious mismatches/indels being called at junction boundaries.

**2. GATK's RNA-seq workflow has one mandatory extra step: SplitNCigarReads.** Reads spanning a splice junction carry an "N" CIGAR operator for the skipped intron. SplitNCigarReads splits these into separate segments and hard-clips overhanging mismatched sequence right at the exon boundary — this single step is the dominant fix for RNA-seq's characteristic alignment-artifact source. Downstream, GATK recommends cluster-based filtering (flag clusters of \~3 variants within a 35bp window) specifically because splice-junction artifacts tend to produce exactly this signature — a real, distinct filter you wouldn't use on DNA-seq.

**3. Coverage tracks expression, not mappability/GC.** In DNA-seq, coverage variation comes from GC bias, mappability, capture efficiency (all covered above). In RNA-seq, a locus only has read support if the gene is transcribed in the sampled tissue/cell type — a silent gene gives you zero power to call anything there, and critically, you cannot distinguish "truly homozygous reference" from "this gene isn't expressed here" the way you can in DNA-seq. This changes what a "negative result" even means.

**4. Allele-specific expression (ASE) breaks the allele-balance logic from your take-home.** A genuinely heterozygous germline SNP can show wildly skewed RNA-seq read ratios (e.g., 90/10 instead of \~50/50) because of imprinting, a cis-regulatory variant affecting one allele's transcription, or nonsense-mediated decay preferentially degrading one allele's transcript — none of that is a genotyping artifact, it's real biology. Applying Step 5's "skewed allele balance = artifact" logic directly to RNA-seq would misclassify real ASE as noise — a good example to raise proactively if asked how your take-home framework would need to change for a different data type.

**5. RNA editing is a false-positive source unique to RNA-seq.** ADAR-mediated A-to-I editing (read as A\>G) and APOBEC-mediated C-to-U editing create real differences between the RNA and the genomic DNA that are not germline or somatic DNA variants at all. These get filtered against known-editing-site databases (REDIportal, DARNED) and recognized by their characteristic single-base-change signature — mechanistically the RNA-seq analog of how 8-oxoG creates a recognizable G\>T signature in DNA-seq, except this one is real post-transcriptional biology, not sequencing chemistry.

**6. Somatic VAF from RNA-seq is systematically distorted for truncating variants.** Nonsense-mediated decay degrades transcripts carrying premature stop codons, so a nonsense/frameshift somatic variant's apparent VAF in RNA-seq will read *lower* than its true DNA-level clonal fraction — a distinct, mechanism-specific caveat beyond the general ASE point above.

**CNV/large-scale copy number from RNA-seq** can't use raw depth the way DNA-seq does, because RNA abundance reflects expression *and* copy number confounded together, not copy number alone. What actually works: smoothing average expression across many genes in genomic order relative to a reference set — a true large-scale (multi-Mb/arm-level) DNA gain or loss shifts the average expression of essentially all genes in that region in the same direction, and averaging across enough genes cancels out each gene's own regulatory noise to reveal the coordinated shift. This is the basis of inferCNV/CopyKAT/HoneyBADGER (built mainly for distinguishing malignant vs. normal cells in tumor single-cell RNA-seq). It only has power for large coordinated events, not small focal CNVs — you need enough expressed genes in the region to average over. Allele-specific/BAF-style signal at heterozygous SNPs captured in RNA-seq reads (same logic as MoChA above) can supplement this, but is itself confounded by the same ASE issue from point 4.

**One thing RNA-seq gives you that DNA-seq doesn't:** gene fusion detection via chimeric/split-read alignment across two different genes (STAR-Fusion, Arriba) — this confirms a fusion is *actually transcribed* (and often whether it's in-frame), which DNA-level SV calling alone can't establish; DNA-seq sees the genomic rearrangement, RNA-seq sees whether it's functionally expressed.

**Bottom line, in one sentence if asked to summarize:** RNA-seq variant/CNV calling is best treated as a secondary, orthogonal evidence layer — confirming expression of a DNA-level call, ASE analysis, splice-variant functional impact, fusion detection — rather than the primary source for germline/somatic small-variant or CNV calling, where WGS/WES remains the gold standard precisely because of the expression-dependent coverage, ASE/NMD confounds, and RNA-editing false positives above.

## 7g. BioMysteryBench-style omics practice questions — solutions + interview framing

Real questions from Anthropic's BioMysteryBench (bioinformatics eval, Apr 2026) and its public sample set. Solutions below are my own grounded technical answers, cross-checked against established methodology — not copied from Anthropic's private answer rubric, which I don't have access to.

**1. What chromosome is triplicated in this sample?** Normalized per-chromosome read depth (\~1.5x vs. diploid baseline) plus B-allele frequency at heterozygous SNPs — trisomy shows a distinctive 3-band BAF split (0, 0.33/0.67, 1.0) instead of the normal 2-band pattern (0, 0.5, 1.0). Interview framing: this is the constitutional, whole-chromosome version of the MoChA LRR+BAF logic covered above — same joint signal, no mosaic cell-fraction scaling to account for.

**2. Which 2 samples in this set are close relatives?** Pairwise kinship/IBD across the cohort (KING, PC-Relate); the pair with kinship ≈0.25 (parent-offspring/full-sib) or ≈0.125 (second-degree) stands out against a background of ≈0 for unrelated pairs.

**3. Given mtDNA WGS from a mother-son pair, identify the number of homoplasmic variants.** Call variants against the rCRS mitochondrial reference, classify each by heteroplasmy level (VAF): homoplasmic ≈100% VAF (fixed across essentially all mtDNA copies), heteroplasmic = intermediate VAF (mixed mutant/wild-type copies). The mother-son framing tests the mitochondrial bottleneck specifically: homoplasmic variants transmit essentially unchanged (maternal inheritance), while heteroplasmy fraction can shift between generations because only a small random sample of mtDNA copies passes through oogenesis. Same allele-balance/VAF-thresholding mechanism as everywhere else in this doc, applied to an extremely high-copy-number, maternally-inherited genome.

**4. Which two samples were swapped in the sequencing run?** Compare "expected identity" (from metadata/pairing — e.g. supposed tumor-normal pairs or same-patient replicates) against "genetic identity" (fingerprint concordance at a polymorphic SNP panel, or full kinship). A swap shows up as two samples that fail to match their expected partner but instead cross-match each other. Sex-check discordance (reported vs. genetically inferred sex) is the other classic tell.

**5. Estimate the haploid genome size to the nearest Mb.** K-mer spectrum method (Jellyfish/KMC counting → GenomeScope-style fitting): genome size ≈ total k-mer count / peak k-mer coverage (the histogram mode, representing true single-copy sequencing depth). Reference-free, works even without an assembly — relevant for non-model organisms, which is a large share of Latch's actual customer base.

**6. This is literally your own Step 6 pipeline, just reporting a coordinate instead of a clinical call — low-effort, high-confidence answer.

**7. Cluster 60 individuals into 5 groups from genotype data.** PCA on genome-wide common variants, then k-means (or similar) on the top PCs — the unsupervised version of the ancestry-assessment answer from earlier (no reference panel to project against, just structure in the data itself).

Framing: this moves from "what mutated" to "why" — SBS1 (clock-like), SBS4 (smoking), SBS7 (UV), SBS3 (HR-deficiency/BRCA) each imply a distinct etiological mechanism, independent of which specific gene is hit. Mechanism-first reasoning — exactly what your own MCQ philosophy rewards.

## 7h. Distinguishing probands and maternal/paternal haplotype from a set of BAMs

**Step 1 — reconstruct family structure.** Pairwise kinship/IBD across every sample (KING-robust, PC-Relate). A trio shows up as one sample with kinship ≈0.25 to two others, with those two typically ≈0 kinship to each other — gives you child vs. parents purely from genotypes, no metadata needed.

**Step 2 — assign maternal vs. paternal identity.** Kinship alone doesn't say which parent is which — that needs sex-typing (X het rate + X:autosome depth + Y coverage, covered earlier) to find which parent is XX (mother) vs. XY (father). Orthogonal confirmations: mtDNA is 100% maternal (child's mtDNA should match mother's exactly), and if the child is male, his Y-haplotype should match the father's almost exactly (barring mutation) — same logic as forensic Y-STR paternity testing.

**Terminology note worth having ready**: "proband" is a clinical/phenotypic designation (the individual through whom a family was clinically ascertained), not something genotype data alone can determine — genetics gives you pedigree structure, not who's "the proband," which needs phenotype/clinical metadata (in practice, often just convention that the child is the proband since trios are usually sequenced because the child is affected). Naming this distinction explicitly signals precision rather than conflating a genetic result with a clinical label.

**Step 3 — parent-of-origin (maternal/paternal) haplotype assignment.** Mendelian/trio-based phasing is deterministic at any site where **at least one parent is homozygous** — that parent's contribution is fixed, so the other allele is assigned to the other parent by subtraction (child 0/1, father 0/0 → child's "1" must be maternal). This works by elimination even when the other parent is heterozygous. The only genuinely ambiguous case is a site where **both parents are heterozygous** — Mendelian logic alone can't resolve parent-of-origin there; falls back to read-backed phasing (WhatsHap, via physical linkage to a nearby resolved site) or population-panel statistical phasing.

**Important distinction**: per-site parent-of-origin labeling isn't the same as a continuous phased haplotype — knowing "this allele is maternal" at each site independently doesn't tell you two maternal alleles at different sites sit on the same physical chromosome copy. WhatsHap's pedigree mode combines trio Mendelian constraints with read-backed phasing simultaneously in one pass, giving both the parent-of-origin label and the longest possible contiguous phase blocks — the actual implementation answer if asked to go beyond the theory.
