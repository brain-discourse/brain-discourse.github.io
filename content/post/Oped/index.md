title: The blue pills that became NYC’s worst nightmare
---

In 2017, 70,237 people died from a drug overdose in the United States, the highest number recorded at the time. New York City lost 1,487 people. Staten Island remained one of the boroughs hardest hit: 101 residents died of overdose, mostly involving opioids, giving it the city’s second-highest borough overdose death rate that year. Infamously labeled “painkiller paradise,” this borough, a 30-minute ferry ride from Manhattan, became a visible symbol of the opioid crisis. This is a public health crisis that transcends age, race, and income. What began as a war on drugs during the Nixon era, shaped in part by the stigmatization and criminalization of people of color, became a national epidemic. Yet the response to opioid use has continued to reflect racism and elitism. White Americans affected by opioids have often been described as victims of a public health crisis, while Black people who use drugs have long been labeled addicts or criminals. This contrast exposes a deep divide in whose suffering is medicalized, whose behavior is punished, and how America’s social hierarchies shape the meaning of addiction.

The opioid crisis is not merely a test of individual willpower; it is a test of whether public health can respond to addiction as a treatable medical condition without reproducing the racism and punishment that shaped the war on drugs. People with opioid use disorder are not simply choosing drugs over jobs, families, or survival; addiction can narrow the conditions under which meaningful choice is possible. If a person’s capacity for voluntary choice is impaired by dependence, coercive social conditions, or lack of access to care, then their apparent consent cannot be treated as fully free. The moralized view of addiction as destructive behavior deserving criminal justice intervention must be replaced by an evidence-based view of opioid use disorder as a treatable condition shaped by neurobiology, social context, trauma, and access to care.

Medications for opioid use disorder, including methadone and buprenorphine, reduce cravings and withdrawal symptoms, lower overdose risk, and support recovery. Rather than simply substituting for illicit opioid use, these evidence-based interventions stabilize patients, reduce harm, and replace an unpredictable, dangerous cycle with safer, medically supervised dosing. When paired with accessible care, counseling, harm-reduction services, and social support, this strategy is both effective and significantly cheaper than relying on inpatient rehabilitation alone. Yet access remains limited: in 2022, only about one-quarter of U.S. adults who needed treatment for opioid use disorder received medication.

New York has already moved toward a public-health response. State and local programs have expanded buprenorphine access in primary care, emergency departments, and correctional facilities; trained peer recovery coaches; supported syringe exchange programs; developed overdose response plans; and created Drug User Health Hubs to connect people who use drugs with culturally competent care. These efforts matter. By the end of 2018, 24 local health departments had trained 134 new buprenorphine providers, and syringe exchange programs had served more than 28,000 clients. But the persistence of overdose deaths shows that public-health tools have not reached everyone who needs them. Expanding medications for opioid use disorder, lowering copayments, integrating treatment into primary care, and bringing services directly into high-burden communities is not leniency; it is prevention.

Although HealingNYC aimed to reduce opioid overdose deaths by 35% over five years, the burden remained concentrated in marginalized communities. Drug overdose is one of the leading causes of premature death, untreated illness, family instability, and preventable harm, predominantly in Staten Island and the Bronx. In 2017, overdose death rates were highest among Black New Yorkers for the first time in 11 years, exposing how poorly the city’s response had reached communities most burdened by criminalization and limited access to care. A bipartisan response should not depend on whether the person affected is framed as a victim or condemned as an addict. It means replacing the language of blame with the language of treatment: expanding medications for opioid use disorder, lowering copayments, integrating care into primary care, and bringing services directly into communities with the highest overdose burden.

Critics may argue that harm reduction allows patients to continue misusing opioids rather than forcing abstinence. But evidence from places such as Sydney, Lisbon, and Vancouver suggests that a public-health model can reduce overdose deaths by removing stigma, offering safe spaces for supervised consumption, and connecting people to treatment rather than pushing drug use into alleys, bathrooms, and isolation. Portugal’s transition from a country once associated with a severe heroin crisis to one frequently cited for low drug-related mortality is a leading example of this approach. Its lesson is not decriminalization alone, but a broader harm-reduction initiative that includes treatment, opioid substitution therapy, sterile supplies, supervised consumption, and easier entry into care. If the goal is to save lives, not punish addiction, overdose prevention centers are a logical extension of public health: they create supervised settings where overdoses can be reversed and people can be connected to treatment. Efforts should be made to decriminalize drug use, reduce stigma, and make treatment and harm-reduction services accessible, especially among marginalized communities. While we cannot completely eradicate opioids, we can save lives if we treat people with opioid use disorder as individuals who need helping hands, not handcuffs.
