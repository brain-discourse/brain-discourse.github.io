summary: Focal epilepsy accounts for roughly 60% of epilepsy cases, and approximately 30% of focal epilepsy is pharmacoresistant. Focal cortical malformations are an important cause of pharmacoresistant focal epilepsy and can arise from post-zygotic, or somatic, variants whose developmental timing shapes their cellular distribution and clinical presentation. This dissertation addresses the gap between detecting brain-restricted somatic variants and interpreting their effects at cellular resolution. Across three aims, it establishes a framework for genotype-resolved interpretation of somatic mosaicism in human brain tissue by capturing rare variant-positive cell populations and integrating DNA, RNA, and orthogonal modalities. Together, these studies move somatic-variant analysis from tissue-based diagnosis toward cell-resolved disease interpretation.
abstract: Epilepsy is a common neurological disorder with a heterogeneous genetic architecture. Although germline variants explain a subset of epilepsy cases, the genetic basis of many focal epilepsies remains poorly understood. In patients with pharmaco-resistant focal epilepsy, surgical resection of epileptogenic brain tissue has enabled direct molecular analysis of affected lesions and has established pathogenic somatic mosaic variants as important contributors to focal malformations of cortical development (MCDs), including hemimegalencephaly (HMEG) and focal cortical dysplasia (FCD). Because somatic variants arise postzygotically and are present in only a subset of cells, their biological consequences cannot be resolved from bulk tissue sequencing alone. Tissue-level studies can identify pathogenic variants, estimate mosaic burden, and implicate recurrent pathways, but they cannot determine which cell types harbor a variant or how genotype relates to transcriptional state within the same cell. As a result, mechanistic interpretation of mosaic epilepsies remains limited. This dissertation addresses these gaps by integrating genomic and transcriptomic measurements across cellular and tissue levels. Chapter 1 reviews somatic mosaicism in brain development and disease, with emphasis on focal MCDs. Chapter 2 uses high-depth exome sequencing of surgically resected HMEG and FCD tissue to identify pathogenic somatic variants and support pathway-level convergence in mTOR-associated lesions. Chapter 3 presents SoMoSeq, a single- nucleus approach that links locus-specific DNA genotyping with full-length transcriptome profiling from the same nucleus. Chapter 4 applies SoMoSeq to HMEG cases carrying recurrent PIK3CA p.E545K variants to map genotype distribution across major cortical cell classes and test for genotype-associated transcriptional differences. Together, these studies support somatic mosaic variation as a major contributor to focal MCD-associated epilepsy and establish a genotype-resolved framework for linking pathogenic variants to cellular identity and transcriptional state in human mosaic brain tissue.
