summary: Dissertation defense presenting a three-part body of work on somatic mosaicism in focal cortical malformations, from cohort-scale variant discovery to SoMoSeq, a genotype-resolved single-nucleus method, and its application to PIK3CA mosaicism in hemimegalencephaly.
abstract: |
  Focal cortical malformations (FCMs) account for roughly 60% of epilepsy cases and up to 30% of pharmacoresistant focal epilepsy, and arise from post-zygotic (somatic) variants whose developmental timing shapes their cellular distribution and clinical presentation. This dissertation addresses the gap between detecting brain-restricted somatic variants and interpreting their effects at cellular resolution, across three aims.

  Aim 1 defined the genetic landscape of FCMs through high-depth exome sequencing of a 186-sample cohort of resected epileptogenic tissue, identifying recurrent somatic variants converging on PI3K-AKT-mTOR pathway hyperactivation across hemimegalencephaly and focal cortical dysplasia type II, and establishing that variant allele frequency reflects developmental timing across FCM subtypes.

  Aim 2 developed and validated SoMoSeq, a genotype-resolved single-nucleus RNA-sequencing method that physically separates DNA and RNA from the same nucleus prior to independent amplification, enabling direct linkage of somatic genotype to transcriptional state. Across 3,316 nuclei, SoMoSeq genotype calls recapitulated bulk allele fractions, and marker-based clustering successfully assigned variant-bearing nuclei across major cortical lineages.

  Aim 3 applied SoMoSeq to PIK3CA-mosaic hemimegalencephaly tissue, showing that PIK3CA variant nuclei span both neuronal and glial lineages - consistent with an early progenitor origin - and that genotype-resolved pathway analysis can separate lesion-associated from genotype-associated transcriptional signals within the same tissue, while identifying allelic dropout in whole-nucleus DNA as a key limitation for resolving loss-of-heterozygosity at the PIK3CA locus.

  Together, this work establishes a framework for genotype-resolved interpretation of somatic mosaicism in human brain tissue: linking genotype and cell type at single-cell resolution, at a scale sufficient to capture rare variant populations, and integrating DNA, RNA, and orthogonal modalities to move from tissue-based diagnosis toward cell-resolved disease interpretation.
